Our Story · Est. 2014

Twelve years of slow hands.

Velo began with one chair, one stylist, and a stubborn belief that hair deserved more time than the industry was giving it. A decade later, we're still holding to that — just with more hands, and better tools.

The Beginning

A single chair on Liberty Street.

Velo opened on a quiet block in 2014 with eight hundred square feet, one styling station, and a borrowed espresso machine. The founder, Marcus, had spent a decade behind bigger chairs — Chicago, Toronto, New York — watching the same scene repeat itself: too many clients, too little time, and a consultation that fit inside a ninety-second walk to the shampoo bowl.

"We wanted to give the craft back its breathing room. That was the whole brief."

Word moved the way it does in a college town — slowly, and then all at once. Within a year there was a waitlist. Within two, Marcus brought on Nia. The studio doubled, added a second chair, and started drafting the house principles that still govern the floor today.

The House Principles

What we won't compromise.

i.

Consultation first

Every service begins with a real conversation — at least ten minutes, sometimes thirty. We want to understand your history before we touch a single strand. There's no faster way to do better work.

ii.

Specialists, not generalists

Our stylists go deep, not wide. One of us lives for fades. Another for balayage. A third for coils. You get matched with the artist whose craft is built for your hair — not whoever has an opening.

iii.

The chair is yours

We never double-book. When your name is on the chair, the stylist is fully yours for the duration — no running between clients, no rushing the finish because the next guest just walked in.

The Craft

Tools we keep sharp.

We sharpen our shears weekly. We invest in the best color line our dollar can buy and replace it when something better comes along. We travel — annually, every senior stylist — to train with master educators in Tokyo, London, Los Angeles, Toronto. When a new technique earns its place, it lives on our floor.

But the most important tool in the studio is the one that costs nothing: the patience to slow down long enough to see clearly. That's the thing we're really trying to teach.

The Studio Today

Seven artists, one standard.

The Velo floor has grown from one chair to seven. Marcus still cuts. Nia oversees stylist development. Elena leads our color bar. David and Julian run the barber program. Sarah shapes waves; Kassa specializes in texture. We're deliberately small, and we intend to stay that way — because the studio only works when every guest knows their stylist's name, and every stylist knows what's happening at the next chair.

"A boutique isn't a size. It's an attention to what matters."
